## General Business Loan FAQs

Use on: Homepage, How It Works, general blog articles

### How do I get a business loan?

Start by determining how much you need and what you'll use it for. Gather basic financial documents like bank statements and tax returns. Then apply through a lender, broker, or platform like Quick Lenders. Most applications take 10-15 minutes, and you can receive offers within 1-3 business days.

### What credit score do I need for a business loan?

Most lenders require a minimum credit score of 600-650 for business loans. However, requirements vary by loan type. SBA loans typically need 680+, while some online lenders work with scores as low as 500. Higher scores get better rates and terms.

### How long does it take to get a business loan?

Timing varies by loan type. Online lenders can fund in 1-3 days. Traditional banks take 2-4 weeks. SBA loans typically take 30-90 days. The fastest funding comes from lines of credit and merchant cash advances, sometimes within 24 hours.

### Can I get a business loan with bad credit?

Yes, but your options are more limited and rates will be higher. Alternative lenders, merchant cash advances, and invoice factoring are more accessible with poor credit. You may also qualify with a strong co-signer or significant collateral.

### How much can I borrow for my business?

Loan amounts depend on your revenue, time in business, credit score, and loan type. Most lenders offer between $5,000 and $5 million. A general rule: you can typically borrow up to 10-15% of annual revenue for unsecured loans, or more with collateral.

### What documents do I need to apply for a business loan?

Standard requirements include: business and personal tax returns (2-3 years), bank statements (3-6 months), profit and loss statement, balance sheet, business licenses, and government-issued ID. Some lenders require less documentation for smaller loans.

### Will applying for a business loan hurt my credit score?

Initial prequalification usually involves a soft credit pull, which doesn't affect your score. A hard pull happens when you formally apply and can lower your score by 5-10 points temporarily. Multiple applications within 14-45 days typically count as one inquiry.

### What can I use a business loan for?

Common uses include equipment purchases, inventory, hiring, marketing, expansion, real estate, refinancing existing debt, and working capital. Most loans don't restrict usage, but some (like equipment financing or real estate loans) must be used for specific purposes.

### How do I know which type of business loan I need?

Consider your purpose, timeline, and how you want to repay. Need ongoing flexibility? Line of credit. Buying equipment? Equipment financing. One-time purchase with predictable repayment? Term loan. Not sure? Our loan finder quiz can help match you with the right option.

### Is a business loan the same as a personal loan?

No. Business loans are for business purposes and typically based on business financials. Personal loans are based on personal credit and income. Using a personal loan for business is possible but may have tax implications and doesn't build business credit.

## Qualification & Requirements FAQs

Use on: Product pages, how-it-works, application checklist, articles about requirements

### What are the basic requirements for a business loan?

Most lenders require: minimum 6-12 months in business, $50,000-$100,000+ annual revenue, 600+ credit score, and a registered business entity. Requirements vary by loan type and lender. Startups and lower-revenue businesses have fewer options but can still qualify.

### Can startups get business loans?

Yes, but options are limited. SBA microloans, some online lenders, and equipment financing work with businesses under 1-2 years old. Startups may need stronger personal credit, a detailed business plan, or collateral to qualify.

### Do I need collateral for a business loan?

Not always. Unsecured loans don't require specific collateral but may require a personal guarantee. Secured loans need collateral like equipment, real estate, inventory, or receivables. Secured loans typically offer lower rates and higher amounts.

### What is a personal guarantee?

A personal guarantee means you're personally responsible for repaying the loan if your business can't. Most small business loans require one. It allows lenders to pursue your personal assets if you default. It's standard practice, not a red flag.

### Can I get a business loan if I've been denied before?

Yes. Denial by one lender doesn't mean all will deny you. Different lenders have different criteria. Address the reason for denial first (improve credit, increase revenue, reduce existing debt), then apply to lenders that fit your profile better.

### How much revenue do I need to qualify?

Minimum revenue requirements range from $50,000 to $250,000+ annually, depending on the lender and loan type. Some alternative lenders accept lower revenue. Higher revenue generally qualifies you for larger loan amounts and better terms.

### Does my industry affect my loan options?

Yes. Some industries are considered higher risk: restaurants, construction, cannabis, firearms, adult entertainment, and gambling-related businesses. Higher-risk industries may face stricter requirements, higher rates, or limited lender options.

### Can I get a business loan for an LLC?

Yes. LLCs, corporations, sole proprietorships, and partnerships can all qualify for business loans. The business structure doesn't determine eligibility, but lenders will review your business financials regardless of entity type.

## Rates & Costs FAQs

Use on: Product pages, comparison articles, calculator pages

### What interest rates can I expect on a business loan?

Rates vary widely by loan type and your qualifications. SBA loans: 6-10%. Bank term loans: 7-15%. Online lenders: 10-35%. Lines of credit: 8-25%. Equipment financing: 6-20%. The rate you receive depends on credit score, revenue, time in business, and loan amount.

### What's the difference between APR and interest rate?

Interest rate is the base cost of borrowing. APR (Annual Percentage Rate) includes the interest rate plus fees, giving you the true yearly cost. Always compare APR, not just interest rates, when evaluating loan offers.

### What fees should I expect when getting a business loan?

Common fees include: origination fee (1-5% of loan amount), application fee ($0-500), underwriting fee, documentation fee, and potentially closing costs. Some lenders charge no fees. Always ask for a complete fee breakdown before accepting.

### Are there prepayment penalties?

Some loans charge a penalty for paying off early, typically 1-5% of the remaining balance. This compensates lenders for lost interest. Many online lenders don't charge prepayment penalties. Always check before signing.

### Why are some business loan rates so high?

Higher rates reflect higher risk. Factors that increase rates: lower credit scores, less time in business, lower revenue, unsecured loans, faster funding, and certain industries. Lower-risk borrowers with strong profiles get the best rates.

### How is my interest calculated?

Most term loans use simple interest on the declining balance. Some alternative lenders use factor rates (a multiplier applied to the principal). Lines of credit charge interest only on what you draw. Ask your lender exactly how interest is calculated.

## Loan Type-Specific FAQs

### Term Loans

**How does a term loan work?**
You receive a lump sum and repay it in fixed installments (monthly, weekly, or daily) over a set period. Each payment includes principal and interest. Once repaid, the loan is closed. Terms range from 1-10 years depending on the loan.

**What's the difference between short-term and long-term business loans?**
Short-term loans (3 months to 2 years) have faster approval, higher rates, and smaller amounts. Long-term loans (3-10 years) offer lower rates and larger amounts but require stronger qualifications and longer approval processes.

**Can I get a term loan for any business purpose?**
Yes. Term loans are flexible and can be used for equipment, expansion, inventory, hiring, marketing, debt consolidation, or general working capital. There are typically no restrictions on use.

### Lines of Credit

**How does a business line of credit work?**
A line of credit gives you access to a set amount of funds you can draw from as needed. You only pay interest on what you borrow. As you repay, funds become available again. It's revolving credit, similar to a credit card.

**What's the difference between a line of credit and a term loan?**
A term loan provides one lump sum with fixed repayment. A line of credit lets you borrow, repay, and borrow again up to your limit. Lines of credit are better for ongoing or unpredictable needs; term loans are better for one-time expenses.

**Do I pay interest if I don't use my line of credit?**
Most lines of credit don't charge interest on unused funds, but some charge a small maintenance or annual fee. You only pay interest when you actually draw funds.

### Equipment Financing

**Can I finance used equipment?**
Yes, though terms may differ. Most lenders finance used equipment that's in good condition and has remaining useful life. You may get lower loan-to-value ratios (70-80% instead of 100%) and shorter terms for older equipment.

**Do I own the equipment at the end of the loan?**
With equipment financing (a loan), yes. You own it once paid off. With equipment leasing, it depends on the lease type. Operating leases return the equipment; capital leases typically include a purchase option.

**What's the difference between equipment financing and equipment leasing?**
Financing is a loan where you own the equipment and build equity. Leasing is renting, often with lower payments but no ownership. Financing is better for long-term use; leasing suits frequently upgraded equipment.

### SBA Loans

**Why are SBA loans harder to get?**
SBA loans have stricter requirements because they offer better terms. You need strong credit (680+), solid financials, 2+ years in business, and must meet SBA size standards. The tradeoff: lower rates and longer terms than most alternatives.

**How long does SBA loan approval take?**
SBA loans typically take 30-90 days from application to funding. SBA Express loans can be faster (within 36 hours for SBA response), but full processing still takes 2-4 weeks. Plan ahead if you need SBA financing.

**What's the difference between SBA 7(a) and 504 loans?**
SBA 7(a) is the most flexible program, usable for most business purposes up to $5 million. SBA 504 is specifically for fixed assets like real estate or major equipment, with even lower rates but more restrictions.

### Asset-Based & Invoice Financing

**What's the difference between invoice factoring and invoice financing?**
Factoring sells your invoices to a third party who collects payment directly from your customers. Financing uses invoices as collateral for a loan while you retain ownership and collect payment yourself. Factoring is faster; financing is more discreet.

**How much can I get for my invoices?**
Factoring companies typically advance 80-90% of invoice value upfront, with the remainder (minus fees) paid when the customer pays. Financing amounts depend on your invoice volume and customer creditworthiness.

**Do my customers know I'm using invoice financing?**
With invoice financing, usually not. With factoring, usually yes, because the factoring company collects payment directly. If discretion matters, choose financing over factoring.

## Process & Timeline FAQs

Use on: How it works, application checklist, "how to apply" articles

### What happens after I apply?

After submitting your application, a lender reviews your information, verifies documents, and may ask follow-up questions. If approved, you'll receive loan offers detailing amounts, rates, terms, and fees. Review, accept, and funding follows.

### How long does approval take?

Online lenders: 24-72 hours. Banks: 1-3 weeks. SBA loans: 30-90 days. Faster funding usually means less documentation required but potentially higher rates. Complex loans take longer due to more thorough underwriting.

### What if I'm approved for less than I need?

You can accept the lower amount, apply with additional lenders, improve your qualifications and reapply later, or combine multiple funding sources. Sometimes accepting a smaller loan and building a repayment history helps you qualify for more later.

### Can I negotiate loan terms?

Sometimes. Larger loans and stronger applicants have more negotiating power. You can often negotiate rates, fees, prepayment penalties, and term length. It doesn't hurt to ask, especially when comparing multiple offers.

### What happens if I can't make a payment?

Contact your lender immediately. Many offer hardship programs, payment deferrals, or modified terms. Ignoring the problem leads to late fees, credit damage, and potential default. Proactive communication usually results in better outcomes.

## Industry-Specific FAQs

### Restaurants

**Can I get a restaurant loan with no experience?**
Limited industry experience makes financing harder. Lenders prefer borrowers with restaurant management or ownership background. You may need stronger financials, a co-signer, or collateral to offset inexperience.

**What's the best financing for restaurant equipment?**
Equipment financing lets the equipment serve as collateral, so it's often easier to qualify for than unsecured loans. Terms typically match equipment useful life (5-7 years). Leasing is also popular for restaurants that upgrade frequently.

### Construction

**How do construction business loans differ from regular business loans?**
Construction lending accounts for project-based cash flow, seasonal fluctuations, and equipment intensity. Lenders want to see a project pipeline, bonding capacity, and equipment lists. Lines of credit are popular for managing cash flow between project payments.

**Can I finance heavy equipment for construction?**
Yes. Equipment financing is common in construction. You can finance excavators, loaders, trucks, and other heavy machinery with the equipment as collateral. Terms typically run 5-7 years with rates starting around 6-8%.

### Trucking

**What do I need to finance a semi truck?**
Most truck financing requires: valid CDL, proof of authority (MC number), 1-2 years driving experience, 620+ credit score, and 10-20% down payment. Requirements are easier for owner-operators with established authority.

**Can new trucking companies get financing?**
It's harder but possible. New trucking companies may need larger down payments (20-30%), higher credit scores, and possibly a co-signer. Some lenders specialize in new authority trucking loans.

### Healthcare

**What financing options exist for medical practices?**
Medical practices can access: practice acquisition loans, equipment financing (for diagnostic equipment, furniture, tech), real estate loans (for buying office space), lines of credit (for cash flow), and SBA loans. Healthcare-focused lenders understand the industry.

**How do I finance a medical practice acquisition?**
Practice acquisition loans typically require: 10-20% down, historical practice financials, a transition plan, and your credentials. SBA 7(a) loans are popular for acquisitions with their favorable terms and longer repayment periods.

## Tool-Specific FAQs

### Loan Payment Calculator

**How accurate is the loan payment calculator?**
The calculator provides estimates based on the inputs you provide. Actual payments vary based on your qualifications, lender, and specific loan terms. Use it for planning and comparison, then get actual quotes for precise numbers.

**Does the calculator include fees?**
The basic calculator shows principal and interest payments. Some fees (origination, closing costs) are often added to the loan amount or paid at closing. Ask lenders for the total cost including all fees.

### DSCR Calculator

**What DSCR do lenders want to see?**
Most lenders require DSCR of 1.25 or higher, meaning your income is 25% more than your debt payments. Some accept 1.1-1.15. SBA loans typically require 1.15+. Higher DSCR means lower risk and potentially better terms.

**What if my DSCR is below 1.0?**
A DSCR below 1.0 means your income doesn't cover your debt payments. This makes traditional financing very difficult. Options include: increasing revenue, reducing expenses, paying down existing debt, or seeking alternative financing that weighs other factors.

### Loan Finder Quiz

**How does the quiz determine my loan type?**
The quiz asks about your funding purpose, amount needed, timeline, time in business, and revenue. Your answers are weighted against loan product criteria to find the best matches. It's a starting point, not a final answer.

**Are quiz results guaranteed?**
No. Quiz results suggest likely matches based on your inputs. Actual approval depends on full underwriting. Think of it as narrowing down options, not pre-approval.

### Break-Even Analyzer

**What does break-even tell me about a loan?**
Break-even shows when your investment pays for itself including financing costs. If buying equipment that generates revenue, break-even tells you how long until that revenue covers the cost of the equipment plus loan interest.

**Should I take a loan if break-even is far out?**
Not necessarily bad. Consider: Will the investment keep generating returns after break-even? Can your cash flow handle payments until then? Is the opportunity cost of not investing higher? Break-even is one factor, not the only decision point.
