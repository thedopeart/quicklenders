**Word count:** 1,500-1,800
**Tools supported:** Factor Rate to APR Calculator (Tier 1), MCA Payback Calculator (Tier 1)

### Outline

**H1: What Is a Factor Rate? How It Works and What It Really Costs**

Opening (80 words): A factor rate is how merchant cash advances and some short-term business loans price their financing. Instead of an annual interest rate, you see a decimal like 1.2 or 1.4. It looks simple, but it hides the true cost. Here's how factor rates work, how they compare to APR, and how to avoid overpaying.

**H2: Factor Rates vs Interest Rates**
- Interest rate: charged on outstanding balance, decreases as you pay down principal
- Factor rate: multiplied by the original amount, cost is fixed regardless of how fast you pay
- This is why a 1.3 factor rate (which sounds like 30%) is often 60%+ APR
- Simple example: borrow $10,000, factor rate 1.3, you repay $13,000 no matter what

**H2: How to Calculate the Cost of a Factor Rate**
- Total cost = Loan amount x Factor rate
- Example: $50,000 x 1.35 = $67,500 total repayment. Cost of borrowing: $17,500.
- The missing variable: time. A 1.35 factor over 6 months is very different from 1.35 over 18 months.
- **Link:** "Use our [factor rate to APR calculator](/tools/factor-rate-to-apr-calculator) to convert any factor rate to an equivalent annual percentage rate"

**H2: How to Convert a Factor Rate to APR**
- Walk through the conversion formula
- Show the same factor rate (1.3) at different payback periods and the resulting APR
- Table: 1.3 factor rate at 3mo = ~200% APR, 6mo = ~100%, 12mo = ~55%, 18mo = ~40%
- This is why payback period matters as much as the factor rate itself
- **Link:** "See how your MCA stacks up with our [MCA payback calculator](/tools/mca-payback-calculator)"

**H2: What's a Good Factor Rate?**
- Range: 1.1 (cheap, rare) to 1.5+ (expensive)
- Under 1.2: strong deal for qualified borrowers
- 1.2-1.3: typical for good profiles
- 1.3-1.4: average, watch the payback period
- 1.4+: expensive, make sure the ROI justifies it
- Compare to typical APRs on business term loans (10-25%) and lines of credit (8-20%)

**H2: Who Uses Factor Rates and Why**
- MCAs, revenue-based financing, some short-term lenders
- Why they prefer factor rates: simpler to calculate for the lender, no amortization, and it makes the cost look lower than APR would
- Not all factor rate products are bad, but you need to understand the true cost

## Article 8: Prepayment Penalties on Business Loans

### Outline

**H1: Prepayment Penalties on Business Loans: What to Know Before Paying Off Early**

Opening (80 words): Paying off a business loan early should save you money. But many loans include prepayment penalties that eat into your savings or eliminate them entirely. Before you make an extra payment or refinance, understand how these penalties work and whether early payoff actually makes financial sense.

**H2: What Is a Prepayment Penalty?**
- A fee lenders charge when you pay off a loan before the scheduled maturity date
- Why lenders charge them: they're counting on that interest income
- Not all loans have them. SBA 7(a) loans, for example, only have them for the first 3 years on loans with 15+ year terms.

**H2: 5 Types of Prepayment Penalties**

**H3: 1. Percentage of Remaining Balance**
- Most common: 2-5% of the outstanding balance
- Example: $200,000 remaining balance, 3% penalty = $6,000

**H3: 2. Remaining Interest Guarantee**
- You owe all remaining scheduled interest regardless of early payoff
- Common with MCAs and some online lenders
- This effectively eliminates any savings from early payoff

**H3: 3. Declining Penalty (Step-Down)**
- Penalty decreases over time: 5% in year 1, 4% in year 2, etc.
- SBA 7(a) uses this structure
- Most favorable for borrowers who can wait

**H3: 4. Yield Maintenance**
- Common in commercial real estate loans
- Compensates lender for the interest rate differential
- Can be very expensive in falling rate environments

**H3: 5. Flat Fee**
- Fixed dollar amount regardless of timing
- Less common, but the simplest to calculate

- **Link:** "Calculate your specific penalty and see if early payoff saves money with our [prepayment penalty calculator](/tools/prepayment-penalty-calculator)"

**H2: When Paying Off Early Still Makes Sense**
- The penalty is less than remaining interest (usually true with declining penalties)
- You're refinancing to a significantly lower rate
- You're selling the business and need to clear the lien
- **Link:** "Compare the savings of refinancing with our [refinance savings calculator](/tools/refinance-savings-calculator)"

**H2: When Early Payoff Doesn't Save Money**
- Remaining interest guarantee (you owe it all anyway)
- Penalty exceeds the interest savings
- You're close to the end of the loan term (penalty may exceed remaining interest)

**H2: How to Avoid Prepayment Penalties**
- Ask about penalties before signing (this is negotiable with some lenders)
- Look for loans with no prepayment penalty (SBA microloans, many lines of credit)
- If the penalty is unavoidable, factor it into your total cost analysis → link to /tools/total-cost-of-capital-calculator
- Read the payoff provision in your loan agreement before signing

### Outline

**H1: What Is DSCR and Why Do Lenders Care About It?**

Opening (80 words): DSCR stands for Debt Service Coverage Ratio. It measures whether your business generates enough income to cover its debt payments. Lenders use it to decide if you qualify and how much you can borrow. A low DSCR means your cash flow is stretched thin. A high one means you have room to take on debt comfortably.

**H2: How to Calculate DSCR**
- Formula: Net Operating Income / Total Debt Service
- Net Operating Income = Revenue - Operating Expenses (before debt payments)
- Total Debt Service = All monthly loan and lease payments
- Example: $25,000 NOI / $18,000 debt payments = 1.39 DSCR
- **Link:** "Calculate yours instantly with our [DSCR calculator](/tools/dscr-calculator)"

**H2: What DSCR Do Lenders Require?**
- SBA loans: 1.15-1.25 minimum
- Bank term loans: 1.25+ preferred
- Online lenders: 1.0-1.15 (more flexible)
- Real estate loans: 1.20-1.35
- Table showing minimum DSCR by loan type

**H2: What Your DSCR Score Means**
- Below 1.0: Business can't cover current debt. Red flag.

**H1: How Much Working Capital Does Your Business Need?**

Opening (80 words): Working capital is the cash your business has available to cover day-to-day operations. Too little and you can't make payroll or buy inventory. Too much sitting idle means you're not investing in growth. Here's how to calculate your working capital needs and figure out if you need financing to bridge the gap.

**H2: What Working Capital Is (and Isn't)**
- Working capital = current assets - current liabilities
- Current assets: cash, accounts receivable, inventory
- Current liabilities: accounts payable, short-term debt, payroll
- It's not the same as profit or revenue

**H2: How to Calculate Your Working Capital Ratio**
- Formula: current assets / current liabilities
- Above 2.0: strong (maybe too much idle cash)
- 1.5-2.0: healthy
- 1.0-1.5: tight, monitor closely
- Below 1.0: more liabilities than assets, needs attention
- **Link:** "Get your exact ratio and cash runway with our [working capital calculator](/tools/working-capital-calculator)"

**H2: The Cash Conversion Cycle**
- How long it takes to turn inventory into cash
- DIO (days inventory outstanding) + DSO (days sales outstanding) - DPO (days payable outstanding)
- A shorter cycle means less working capital needed
- Example: if your cycle is 60 days, you need 2 months of expenses in working capital

**H2: How Much Is Enough? The 3 Benchmarks**
- **Minimum:** 1 month of operating expenses in cash
- **Comfortable:** 3 months of operating expenses
- **Strong:** 6 months (ideal for seasonal businesses)
- **Link:** "Forecast your cash needs over the next 12 months with our [cash flow forecast tool](/tools/cash-flow-forecast-tool)"

**H2: When to Finance Working Capital**
- Seasonal businesses preparing for slow months
- Growing businesses that need to hire or buy inventory before revenue catches up
- Businesses with long receivable cycles (30-90 day payment terms)
- Recommended product: business line of credit (pay interest only on what you use)
- **Link:** "See what a line of credit would cost with our [LOC interest calculator](/tools/line-of-credit-interest-calculator)"
- → link to /business-loans/lines-of-credit

**H2: Working Capital Mistakes to Avoid**
- Using long-term loans for short-term needs (overpaying on interest)
- Ignoring seasonal patterns until it's too late
- Confusing revenue with cash in hand
- Not separating business and personal finances

**H1: Should You Refinance Your Business Loan? How to Decide**

Opening (80 words): Refinancing replaces your current loan with a new one at better terms. When rates drop or your business profile improves, refinancing can save thousands in interest. But closing costs, prepayment penalties, and resetting the clock on your loan can erase those savings. Here's how to run the numbers and decide if refinancing makes sense.

**H2: When Refinancing Makes Sense**
- Your credit has improved significantly since the original loan
- Market rates have dropped
- You originally took a high-rate loan because you needed speed and can now afford to shop
- You want to consolidate multiple loans into one payment
- You need to change your payment structure (lower monthly payment, different term)

**H2: When Refinancing Doesn't Make Sense**
- You're near the end of your loan term (most interest has been paid)
- Prepayment penalties exceed the savings
- Your business profile hasn't improved (you'll get the same or worse rate)
- The new loan has higher total fees

**H2: How to Calculate Refinance Savings**
- Step 1: Total remaining cost of current loan (remaining payments x payment amount)
- Step 2: Total cost of new loan (all payments + fees + penalty on old loan)
- Step 3: Subtract. If new loan total is lower, refinancing saves money.
- The break-even point: how many months until savings offset costs
- **Link:** "Run the numbers with our [refinance savings calculator](/tools/refinance-savings-calculator)"

**H2: Don't Forget Prepayment Penalties**
- Check your current loan agreement for penalty terms
- Some loans (MCAs, some online lenders) charge full remaining interest
- SBA 7(a) penalties: only for loans 15+ years, only in first 3 years
- **Link:** "Calculate your prepayment penalty with our [prepayment penalty calculator](/tools/prepayment-penalty-calculator)"

**H2: Refinance Costs to Factor In**
- Origination fee on new loan (0-3%)
- Closing costs
- Appraisal fees (for real estate loans)
- Legal fees
- Time cost (SBA refinance takes weeks)
- **Link:** "See the all-in cost of your new loan with our [total cost of capital calculator](/tools/total-cost-of-capital-calculator)"

**H2: Types of Business Loan Refinancing**
- Rate-and-term refinance (new rate, same or different term)
- Cash-out refinance (borrow more than you owe, pocket the difference)
- Debt consolidation (combine multiple loans into one)
- SBA debt refinance program

**H2: How to Refinance**
- Check your current loan terms (payoff amount, penalties)
- Know your current credit score and financials
- Get 2-3 quotes from different lenders
- Compare total cost, not just rate
