Getting a business loan for the first time can feel overwhelming. Lenders want to see that you can repay the money, and without a track record of business borrowing, you need to prove that in other ways. The good news? Options exist for nearly every business situation, and understanding the landscape before you apply dramatically improves your odds of approval.

![Business professionals discussing loan options](/assets/images/site/term-loans.jpg)

## Understanding How Business Lending Works

Unlike personal loans where lenders primarily evaluate you as an individual, business financing considers both you and your company as separate entities. Lenders assess risk based on multiple factors, and first-time borrowers typically face more scrutiny simply because they lack a borrowing history. This isn't a barrier—it just means you need to prove creditworthiness through other means.

The business lending market has expanded significantly over the past decade. Traditional bank loans remain an option for well-qualified borrowers, but online lenders, credit unions, and SBA-backed programs have created pathways for businesses that might have been shut out in the past. The key is understanding which lenders serve borrowers like you, then positioning your application accordingly.

## What Lenders Look For in First-Time Borrowers

When you lack a business credit history, lenders shift their attention to other indicators of your ability to repay. Understanding these evaluation criteria helps you strengthen your application before you submit it.

![Investment and lending strategy meeting](/assets/images/site/investment-banking.jpg)

### Personal Credit History

For first-time business borrowers, your personal credit carries significant weight. Most traditional lenders want to see a score above 680, while scores of 720 or higher typically qualify for the best rates. Your credit report tells lenders how you handle financial obligations—they examine your payment history, how much of your available credit you're using, how long you've maintained accounts, and whether you've been shopping around for credit recently.

That said, options exist even if your credit isn't pristine. Many alternative lenders work with scores as low as 500, though you'll pay higher rates. If your score needs improvement before you apply, taking a few months to [improve your credit](/financial-insights/how-to-improve-credit-fast) can save you thousands in interest over the life of your loan.

### Time in Business

Traditional lenders generally prefer businesses with at least two years of operating history. This track record demonstrates staying power and provides revenue data for analysis. If your business is younger, you'll need to explore alternatives: online lenders that accept businesses with as little as six months of operation, SBA microloans designed specifically for startups, revenue-based financing if you have consistent cash flow, or [equipment financing](/business-loans/equipment-financing) where the equipment itself serves as collateral.

### Revenue and Cash Flow

Lenders want proof that your business generates enough income to cover loan payments comfortably. Most traditional term loans require at least $100,000 in annual revenue, though some alternative lenders set lower thresholds around $50,000. Beyond the raw numbers, lenders examine your cash flow patterns—consistent monthly deposits suggest a stable operation, while erratic swings may raise concerns.

Revenue requirements typically scale with loan amounts. A loan under $100,000 might require annual revenue of $100,000 to $250,000, while loans above $500,000 often require $1 million or more in yearly revenue.

### Industry Considerations

Some industries face more lending restrictions due to perceived risk. Healthcare practices, professional services, and established retail operations generally receive favorable treatment. Restaurants, construction, and seasonal businesses face more scrutiny because of their higher failure rates. Certain sectors—including cannabis in many states, gambling, and adult entertainment—may find traditional lending options extremely limited.

Understanding where your industry falls on the risk spectrum helps set realistic expectations and guides you toward lenders who specialize in your sector.

## Loan Options for First-Time Borrowers

### Term Loans

A term loan provides a lump sum you repay over a fixed period with interest. [Business term loans](/business-loans/term-loans) typically range from $50,000 to $10 million, with repayment terms of one to five years. This structure works well for specific investments like expansion projects, large inventory purchases, or significant hiring initiatives. Interest rates vary widely based on qualifications—anywhere from 7% to 30%—and funding speed ranges from a few days with online lenders to several weeks with traditional banks.

### Business Lines of Credit

A [business line of credit](/business-loans/lines-of-credit) offers flexibility that term loans can't match. You receive access to a set amount of capital and draw funds only when needed, paying interest solely on what you borrow. This makes lines of credit ideal for managing cash flow gaps, handling seasonal fluctuations, or capitalizing on unexpected opportunities. Rates typically run between 10% and 25% APR.

### SBA Loans

The Small Business Administration backs loans through approved lenders, which translates to lower rates and longer terms for borrowers who qualify. The SBA 7(a) program is most common for first-time borrowers, offering loans up to $5 million with terms up to 25 years for real estate or 10 years for equipment. Rates run at prime plus 2.25% to 4.75%. The catch? Extensive documentation requirements and an approval process that typically takes two to three months.

![Equipment and business asset financing](/assets/images/site/equipment-financing.jpg)

### Equipment Financing

If you need specific equipment, [equipment financing](/business-loans/equipment-financing) uses the equipment itself as collateral. This arrangement can make approval significantly easier since the lender has a physical asset backing the loan. You can typically finance up to 100% of the equipment cost, with terms that match the equipment's useful life. Rates range from 4% to 20% depending on your credit profile and the type of equipment.

### Invoice Financing

B2B companies with outstanding invoices from creditworthy customers can leverage that pending revenue through [invoice financing](/financial-insights/invoice-financing). Lenders advance 80% to 90% of invoice value, then collect payment directly from your customers. Since approval depends more on your customers' creditworthiness than yours, this option works well for businesses with limited credit history but strong client relationships.

### Asset-Based Lending

[Asset-based loans](/business-loans/asset-backed-loans) use your business assets—inventory, equipment, real estate, or receivables—as collateral. You can typically borrow 70% to 80% of your assets' value. This opens doors for businesses with valuable assets but limited operating history or cash flow challenges.

## Preparing Your Application

### Review Your Credit Reports

Before applying anywhere, pull your personal credit reports from all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) and review them carefully. Look for errors or inaccuracies and dispute them immediately—mistakes happen more often than you'd expect, and a single error can cost you points. Check for outstanding collections, high credit utilization, or recent late payments that might concern lenders. If you have a DUNS number, also check your business credit with Dun & Bradstreet.

### Gather Your Documentation

Lenders will ask for personal documents including two to three years of tax returns, several months of bank statements, photo ID, and proof of address. On the business side, prepare your business tax returns (if available), bank statements covering at least three to six months, profit and loss statements, balance sheets, business licenses, formation documents, and any lease agreements.

Newer businesses should also prepare a detailed business plan, financial projections, accounts receivable and payable aging reports, and any customer contracts or purchase orders that demonstrate future revenue.

![Bonds and long-term business financing](/assets/images/site/bonds.jpg)

### Calculate Your True Need

Borrowing more than necessary costs you in interest and strains your cash flow. Borrowing too little might leave you short of your goal, potentially worse off than when you started. Calculate the specific amount needed for your purpose, add a cushion of 10% to 20% for unexpected costs, then verify your cash flow can comfortably handle the payments. Don't forget to factor in the total cost of borrowing—interest plus all fees—when making your decision.

### Research and Compare Lenders

Different lenders serve different borrower profiles. Banks and credit unions offer the best rates but have stricter requirements (typically 680+ credit and two or more years in business) and slower approval processes. Online lenders provide faster funding with more flexible requirements, though rates tend to be higher. SBA lenders offer government-backed loans with favorable terms but require extensive documentation and patience.

Get at least three quotes before committing. Compare the true cost including all fees, not just the interest rate. Pay attention to prepayment penalties, personal guarantee requirements, collateral needs, and repayment schedules.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

First-time borrowers often apply to multiple lenders simultaneously without realizing each application can trigger a hard credit inquiry that lowers their score. Use pre-qualification tools that only require soft pulls, or work with a broker who can submit to multiple lenders with a single inquiry.

Many borrowers focus exclusively on interest rate while ignoring fees, prepayment penalties, and other terms that affect total cost. A loan with a lower rate but higher fees might cost more overall. Review every term carefully, especially personal guarantee clauses that make you personally liable if the business can't pay, UCC filings that place liens on business assets, and default clauses that specify what triggers default and its consequences.

Another common mistake is neglecting to build business credit for future borrowing. While personal credit matters most for first-time borrowers, establishing business credit now helps with future financing. Register for a DUNS number, open business credit cards, and work with vendors who report to business credit bureaus.

## Moving Forward

If you're concerned about qualification, consider starting with a smaller loan amount to build credibility, offering collateral to reduce lender risk, finding a creditworthy co-signer, or building relationships with local banks and credit unions before you need to borrow.
