Not every business funding need requires a multi-year loan. Sometimes you need capital quickly to cover a seasonal inventory purchase, bridge a gap between receivables, or handle an unexpected expense. Short-term business financing is designed for exactly these situations, providing funds you repay within a few months to a year.

This comprehensive guide covers all the major short-term financing options, their costs, qualification requirements, and how to choose the right one for your situation.

## What Qualifies as Short-Term Financing?

Short-term business financing generally refers to any funding arrangement with a repayment period of 3 to 18 months. These products are built for speed and flexibility rather than long-term capital investment.

Because repayment happens quickly, monthly (or even daily/weekly) payments tend to be higher than what you would see with a [long-term business loan](/financial-insights/long-term-business-loans). However, the total interest paid is often lower since the balance does not accrue interest over many years.

### Short-Term vs. Long-Term Financing

| Factor | Short-Term | Long-Term |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Repayment period | 3-18 months | 2-25 years |
| Payment frequency | Weekly/daily common | Monthly |
| Approval speed | 1-3 days typical | 2-8 weeks typical |
| Credit requirements | Often lower | Generally higher |
| Interest rates | Higher APR | Lower APR |
| Total interest paid | Lower (shorter duration) | Higher (longer duration) |
| Best for | Working capital, emergencies | Major investments, expansion |

## Common Short-Term Financing Options

### Business Lines of Credit

A [line of credit](/business-loans/lines-of-credit) gives you a revolving pool of capital you can draw from as needed. You only pay interest on the amount you actually use. This makes it ideal for managing cash flow fluctuations or covering expenses that vary from month to month.

**How it works:**
- Approved for a maximum credit limit (e.g., $100,000)
- Draw funds as needed, repay, draw again
- Interest only accrues on outstanding balance
- Many lines are revolving and renew annually

**Typical terms:**
- Credit limits: $10,000 to $500,000+
- Interest rates: 10-25% APR
- Draw period: 12-24 months, often renewable
- Repayment: Monthly minimum payments

**Best for:** Ongoing working capital needs, seasonal fluctuations, opportunity purchases, managing payroll timing

### Merchant Cash Advances (MCA)

A merchant cash advance provides a lump sum in exchange for a percentage of your future credit card or debit card sales. Repayment adjusts automatically based on your daily transaction volume.

**How it works:**
- Receive a lump sum (e.g., $50,000)
- A fixed percentage (10-20%) of daily card sales is withheld
- Continues until you've repaid the purchased amount plus fees
- No fixed repayment timeline—faster sales = faster payoff

**Typical terms:**
- Funding amounts: $5,000 to $500,000
- Factor rates: 1.1 to 1.5 (multiply advance by factor for total repayment)
- Holdback percentage: 10-20% of daily sales
- Effective APR: Often 40-150%+ when calculated

**Best for:** Businesses with consistent card sales needing quick cash, businesses that prefer variable payments matching revenue

**Watch out for:** MCAs are among the most expensive financing options. A factor rate of 1.4 means repaying $70,000 on a $50,000 advance. Always calculate the effective APR before proceeding.

### Invoice Financing and Factoring

If your business has outstanding invoices from creditworthy customers, you can borrow against that unpaid revenue. There are two main structures:

**Invoice Financing (Borrowing):**
- You receive a loan using invoices as collateral
- Retain responsibility for collecting from customers
- Pay interest on the advance until invoices are paid
- Rates: 1-5% of invoice value per month

**Invoice Factoring (Selling):**
- You sell invoices to the factoring company at a discount
- The factor handles collection directly
- Receive 80-90% upfront, remainder (minus fees) when customer pays
- Fees: 1-5% of invoice value

Both options convert future revenue into immediate working capital. Learn more in our detailed [invoice financing guide](/financial-insights/invoice-financing).

**Best for:** B2B companies with long payment cycles (net-30, net-60, net-90 customers), businesses with creditworthy customers but tight cash flow

### Bridge Loans

Bridge loans fill short-term funding gaps, often while a business waits for longer-term financing to close or a large receivable to arrive.

**Common uses:**
- Covering operations while awaiting SBA loan approval
- Purchasing inventory before peak season
- Acquiring property before selling an existing asset
- Meeting payroll during a cash crunch

**Typical terms:**
- Loan amounts: $50,000 to several million
- Terms: 3-12 months
- Rates: 12-24% APR
- Often require collateral or personal guarantee

**Best for:** Temporary gaps with a clear exit strategy, situations where long-term financing is already in progress

### Short-Term Term Loans

A straightforward lump-sum loan with a fixed repayment schedule of 3 to 12 months. [Term loans](/business-loans/term-loans) in this category are typically used for specific one-time expenses.

**How it works:**
- Receive full loan amount upfront
- Fixed payment schedule (usually weekly or monthly)
- Clear payoff date
- May be secured or unsecured

**Typical terms:**
- Loan amounts: $25,000 to $500,000
- Terms: 3-18 months
- Rates: 15-80% APR (varies widely by lender)
- Payments: Often weekly or bi-weekly

**Best for:** Specific, one-time investments—purchasing inventory, funding a marketing campaign, equipment that doesn't qualify for dedicated equipment financing

## When Short-Term Financing Makes Sense

### Seasonal Inventory Purchases

Retailers and other seasonal businesses often need to stock up before peak periods. A short-term loan finances the inventory, which is then sold during the busy season. Revenue from sales covers the repayment.

**Example:** A holiday decor retailer needs $200,000 for inventory in September. They take a 6-month term loan, sell inventory October-December, and repay by February from holiday sales proceeds.

### Cash Flow Timing Gaps

If there's a mismatch between when you pay suppliers and when customers pay you, short-term financing smooths out the gap.

**Example:** A construction company pays subcontractors weekly but doesn't receive payment from clients for 60 days. A line of credit bridges the gap, drawn down for payroll and repaid when client payments arrive.

### Emergency Expenses

Equipment breakdowns, facility repairs, or other urgent costs that cannot wait for a traditional loan approval process.

**Example:** A restaurant's walk-in cooler fails mid-summer. A merchant cash advance provides $30,000 within 48 hours for emergency replacement, avoiding spoiled inventory and lost revenue.

### Time-Sensitive Opportunities

A great deal on inventory, a sudden spike in orders, or a chance to take on a large contract may justify quick financing.

**Example:** A retailer gets offered excess inventory from a closing competitor at 40% below wholesale. A short-term loan finances the purchase, with significant profit margins covering the borrowing costs.

## Qualification Requirements

Short-term lenders tend to have less stringent requirements than traditional banks because they're lending for shorter periods and often take on higher risk for higher returns.

### Typical Requirements

| Factor | Minimum Requirement |
|--------|-------------------|
| Credit Score | 550-650+ (varies by lender) |
| Time in Business | 3-12 months minimum |
| Monthly Revenue | $8,000-$15,000+ |
| Bank Statements | 3-6 months required |

### What Lenders Evaluate

**Cash flow is king.** Short-term lenders focus heavily on your bank statements and consistent deposits. They want to see:
- Regular deposits indicating steady revenue
- Positive average daily balance
- Minimal overdrafts or NSF charges
- Revenue trends (stable or growing preferred)

**Industry matters.** Some industries face restrictions or higher rates:
- Higher risk: Restaurants, construction, retail, transportation
- Lower risk: Healthcare, professional services, B2B services
- Often restricted: Cannabis, gambling, adult entertainment

**Existing debt load.** Lenders calculate your debt service coverage ratio—can your cash flow handle existing payments plus the new one?

## Understanding the True Cost

Short-term financing costs are expressed differently depending on the product, which makes comparison tricky.

### APR (Annual Percentage Rate)

Common for lines of credit and traditional short-term loans. Represents the true annual cost including interest and fees.

**Example:** A $50,000 loan with 24% APR repaid over 6 months costs approximately $6,000 in interest.

### Factor Rate

Used for merchant cash advances and some short-term loans. A multiplier applied to the advance amount.

**Example:** Factor rate of 1.3 on a $50,000 advance means you repay $65,000 total—that's $15,000 in fees regardless of how quickly you repay.

The catch: Factor rates look deceptively low. A factor rate of 1.3 over 6 months equals roughly 60% APR. Over 3 months, it's approximately 120% APR.

### Calculating True Cost

Always calculate the total cost of borrowing:

**Total Cost = (Total Repayment - Amount Received) / Amount Received × 100%**

Then annualize to compare:

**Approximate APR = Total Cost Percentage × (365 / Days to Repay)**

### Fee Types to Watch

- **Origination fees:** 1-5% of loan amount, often deducted upfront
- **Closing fees:** Similar to origination, charged at funding
- **Draw fees:** For lines of credit, charged each time you draw funds
- **Prepayment penalties:** Some lenders charge for early payoff
- **Late fees:** Additional charges for missed payments
- **ACH fees:** Charges for automated payment processing

## Tips for Smart Short-Term Borrowing

### 1. Borrow Only What You Need

Higher payments on short timelines can strain cash flow if you take more than necessary. Calculate your precise requirement and add a small buffer—not more.

### 2. Have a Clear Repayment Plan

Know exactly how the capital will generate the revenue needed to repay on time. Short-term financing for a marketing campaign should produce measurable return. Financing inventory should result in sales.

### 3. Compare Multiple Lenders

Rates and terms vary dramatically. Get at least 3 quotes. Use online marketplaces to streamline comparison, but also check with your bank—existing relationships sometimes yield better terms.

### 4. Read the Agreement Carefully

Pay attention to:
- Daily vs. monthly payment schedules
- Prepayment penalties (some charge even if you pay early)
- Automatic renewal clauses
- Personal guarantee requirements
- UCC filings and collateral liens

### 5. Build Toward Better Terms

Successfully repaying short-term financing strengthens your business credit profile, qualifying you for longer terms and lower rates in the future. Some lenders offer graduating rates for repeat borrowers.

### 6. Consider the Opportunity Cost

Sometimes the "cost" of financing is justified by what it enables. If a $50,000 MCA at 1.4 factor rate lets you fulfill a $200,000 contract, the math may work even with expensive financing.

## Red Flags to Watch For

**Guaranteed approval claims.** Legitimate lenders evaluate risk. "Guaranteed" usually means high fees.

**Pressure to sign quickly.** Reputable lenders give you time to review terms.

**Unclear fee structures.** If you can't easily calculate total cost, be wary.

**Extreme daily payment requirements.** Daily ACH debits that strain your operating cash are a sign of problematic financing.

**Stacking concerns.** Multiple short-term loans at once (called "stacking") can create a debt spiral. Avoid lenders who encourage this.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How quickly can I get short-term financing?

Many short-term lenders fund within 1-3 business days. Merchant cash advances and some online lenders can fund same-day in urgent situations. Bank-based lines of credit typically take 1-2 weeks.

### Will applying hurt my credit score?

Most short-term lenders perform a soft credit check for pre-qualification (no impact). A hard inquiry occurs when you formally apply, which may lower your score by a few points temporarily. However, some lenders—particularly MCAs—don't report to credit bureaus, which also means the positive payment history won't help build your credit.

### Can I pay off early to save money?

It depends on the structure. Loans with APR-based interest: yes, you'll save on interest. Products with factor rates: usually no savings—you owe the fixed amount regardless of timing. Always check for prepayment penalties.

### What happens if I can't make a payment?

Contact your lender immediately. Options may include:
- Payment deferrals
- Modified payment schedules
- Refinancing into different terms

Defaulting can result in collections activity, damage to credit (if reported), and potential legal action. Some lenders have personal guarantee provisions that put your personal assets at risk.

### Can startups qualify for short-term financing?

Yes, though options are more limited. Some lenders accept businesses with as little as 3 months of operating history. Revenue-based financing and merchant cash advances tend to be more accessible than traditional term loans for young businesses. However, expect to pay higher rates. ## Alternatives to Consider

Before committing to short-term financing, consider whether these alternatives might work:

**Business credit cards:** Often offer 0% intro APR periods and are more flexible than term loans.

**Vendor payment terms:** Negotiate net-30 or net-60 terms with suppliers instead of paying upfront.

**Customer deposits:** Require partial payment upfront on large orders.

**[Long-term loans](/financial-insights/long-term-business-loans):** If you need larger amounts for lasting investments, longer terms mean lower payments.

**[Equipment financing](/business-loans/equipment-financing):** For equipment purchases, dedicated financing often offers better rates than general short-term loans.
